Lecture 1 ‚Äî Definition & Meaning of Human Rights

Source: Human rights (first Lecture).

Definition

‚ÄúThe basic rights and freedoms to which all human beings are entitled, often held to include the right to life and liberty, freedom of thought and expression, and equality before the law.‚Äù

Core ideas
  • Human rights are based on respect for the individual and the assumption that each person deserves to be treated with dignity.
  • They are universal: human rights apply to everyone, regardless of group membership.
  • The scope is broad: rights cover choice, opportunity, work, family life, travel, leisure, and protection from abuse or arbitrary dismissal.
The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)

The UDHR is the world‚Äôs premier human rights instrument; its opening paragraph affirms the "inherent dignity and of the equal and inalienable rights of all members of the human family" as the foundation of freedom, justice and peace in the world. The lecture highlights the gap between the UDHR ideals and realities on the ground, stressing the work of advocates to bridge that gulf.

The Cyrus Cylinder (539 B.C.)

After Cyrus the Great conquered Babylon, he issued decrees recorded on a baked-clay cylinder. These included freeing slaves, allowing people to choose their religion, and establishing principles of racial equality. The cylinder is often called the world‚Äôs first charter of human rights and parallels early Articles of the UDHR.

The Magna Carta (1215)

Signed after King John‚Äôs abuses, the Magna Carta enumerated rights such as the church‚Äôs independence from state interference, property and inheritance rights for free citizens, protection from excessive taxation, due process, and equality before the law. It marks a crucial turning point toward modern democracy.

Petition of Right (1628)

Produced by the English Parliament and sent to Charles I, the Petition of Right asserted key civil liberties: no taxation without Parliament consent; no imprisonment without cause (habeas corpus); no quartering of soldiers on citizens; and prohibition of martial law in peacetime.
